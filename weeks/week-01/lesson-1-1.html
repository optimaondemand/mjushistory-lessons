Lesson 1.1
Europe Reaches West
Lesson · ~750 words
📖 Today's Focus
Columbus did not prove the world was round. He won an argument about how big it was, and he won it by being wrong in a lucky way.

By January of 1492, the Spanish crown had just finished a seven-hundred-year project: the conquest of Granada, the last Muslim kingdom in Spain, completed on January 2. Nine months later, the same crown funded a Genoese sailor's plan to sail west into open ocean until he reached Asia. Neither of those two facts caused the other in any simple way. But without the first, it is hard to imagine the second happening when it did. That kind of hinge, where one contingent event opens a door for another, is something you should watch for all semester.

Why Anyone Was Looking West at All

Educated Europeans in 1492 already knew the earth was a sphere; that had been common knowledge among scholars since antiquity. What they argued about was how big that sphere was, and therefore how far a ship would have to sail west to reach Asia. For over a century before Columbus, European interest in reaching Asia by sea had been building for a concrete reason: the overland spice and silk trade ran through the eastern Mediterranean, and after the Ottoman Empire captured Constantinople in 1453, that route grew more expensive and less certain for European merchants. A sea route around the problem became worth a great deal of money to whoever found it first.

Portugal had been chasing that sea route for decades, sending ship after ship down the coast of Africa under the sponsorship of Prince Henry, known to later writers as "the Navigator." That patient, government-funded program paid off in 1488, when the Portuguese captain Bartolomeu Dias rounded the southern tip of Africa, proving a sea route to the Indian Ocean, and therefore to Asia, was possible. New tools made voyages like this survivable: the caravel, the magnetic compass, and the astrolabe, an instrument for measuring a ship's position by the stars.

💡 Helpful to Know
Dias's 1488 voyage matters for a reason you might not expect: it gave Portugal's King John II a good reason to say no to Columbus. Portugal already had a working plan to reach Asia around Africa. Why would the king gamble money on an unproven route across open ocean when a proven one was already in hand? Columbus was rejected by the one court most likely to be interested, for the most reasonable of reasons.

An Argument Won by Being Wrong

Columbus's own plan rested on a serious miscalculation. Using an old, small estimate of the earth's circumference (going back to the ancient geographer Marinus of Tyre) rather than the more accurate figure that other scholars of his own day preferred, Columbus concluded that Asia lay only about 2,400 miles west of the Canary Islands. The true distance is closer to 12,000 miles. Experts who reviewed his proposal, first for Portugal, later for Spain, rejected it again and again, and on the math, they were right to.

Here is the contingent twist: had Columbus been correct about the distance, his plan would never have been funded, because the true 12,000-mile distance was known by better-informed geographers to be impossibly far for a fifteenth-century ship carrying only a few months of food and water. And had he been wrong about the distance and there been no land in between, his crew would have starved in open ocean long before sighting Asia. Columbus's voyage worked only because two separate errors happened to cancel each other out: he underestimated the size of the earth, and he did not know an entire pair of continents sat in the ocean he intended to cross. Neither he nor anyone in Europe had any way of knowing that second fact in 1492.

After years of lobbying and repeated rejection at the Portuguese and then the Spanish court, Columbus finally secured backing from Isabella of Castile and Ferdinand of Aragon in April 1492: monarchs who, having just completed the conquest of Granada, had a treasury and a court newly free to consider his request. Persistence had put him in front of the right people at the right moment; it took the Reconquista's completion to make that moment arrive when it did.

The Crossing

Columbus's three ships, the Niña, the Pinta, and the Santa María, departed the Spanish port of Palos on August 3, 1492, and stopped at the Canary Islands to resupply and pick up the reliable westward trade winds. From there they sailed into open ocean for five weeks, far longer than any of the crew had ever been out of sight of land, and unease turned to something closer to mutiny by early October. On October 12, a lookout aboard the Pinta sighted land: an island in the Bahamas that its Lucayan inhabitants called Guanahaní, which Columbus renamed San Salvador.

Columbus never accepted that he had found something other than the outer edge of Asia. He called the people he met "Indians," a name that stuck for the next several centuries, and on his later voyages he continued to interpret every new discovery as further confirmation of a theory his own crew had begun to doubt. That stubborn self-confidence is worth sitting with as a character trait, not just a mistake: the same refusal to accept "no" that carried a rejected proposal through a decade of European courts to a funded voyage also blinded its author to the plain evidence in front of him once he arrived. Virtue and vice, in the classical sense this course uses those words, are often the same disposition pointed in different directions depending on the circumstances.
