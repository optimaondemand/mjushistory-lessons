Lesson 9.1
The Ohio Country Becomes a Flashpoint
Lesson · ~780 words
📖 Today's Focus
Three parties claimed the Ohio Country in the 1750s. Only two of them were European.

By the 1750s, the Ohio River Valley sat at the intersection of incompatible claims. France considered it part of a connected fur-trading network linking Canada to Louisiana along the Mississippi. Virginia's colonial government, backing land speculators in the Ohio Company, considered it available for British settlement under its own colonial charter. And the Ohio Country's actual inhabitants (the Shawnee, the Delaware (Lenape), Mingo settlers, and others, along with the more distant but still-influential Iroquois Confederacy) had their own interests in the territory that neither European claim had any real right to override, whatever each European power's paperwork said.

A Young Officer's Mission

In late 1753, Virginia's Governor Robert Dinwiddie sent 21-year-old Major George Washington, a young officer with no combat experience, to deliver a formal ultimatum demanding French forces withdraw from the Ohio Country. Washington's mission failed diplomatically (the French politely refused), but it gave him firsthand knowledge of the region's geography and its Native leadership, including Tanaghrisson, a Mingo leader known to the English as the "Half King," who had his own reasons, independent of British interests, for wanting to check growing French influence over the region's Native nations.

Jumonville Glen: The Skirmish That Started a War

In May 1754, Washington led a small force back into the Ohio Country and ambushed a French scouting party at a site called Jumonville Glen, killing several soldiers including the French commanding officer, Joseph Coulon de Jumonville. Accounts differ on precisely what happened next: some sources indicate that Tanaghrisson personally killed the wounded Jumonville during the confused aftermath, for his own political reasons related to Mingo relations with the French rather than any order from Washington. Whatever exactly occurred, French authorities treated the killing as murder rather than a legitimate act of war, and the incident escalated rapidly. Washington's small, hastily built post, Fort Necessity, fell to French and Native forces the following July, forcing his surrender: a humiliating start to a military career that would, decades later, culminate very differently.

💡 Helpful to Know
Notice how much of this war's opening chapter turns on the specific decisions and relationships of a handful of individuals: a young, inexperienced Virginia officer, a Mingo leader pursuing his own diplomatic strategy, a French officer killed in disputed circumstances. None of these people were trying to start a global war. The Seven Years' War that followed was not planned by anyone at Jumonville Glen; it grew out of a chain of specific, contingent decisions that could plausibly have gone differently at almost every step.
