Slavery Before the Atlantic
Lesson · ~780 words
📖 Today's Focus
To understand what was genuinely new about the Atlantic slave trade, you first have to understand what was not new about slavery.

Slavery is one of the oldest and most widespread human institutions, practiced across Asia, Europe, and Africa for thousands of years before Columbus's voyage. Long-distance trade routes connecting sub-Saharan Africa to the Mediterranean and the Middle East, especially the trans-Saharan caravan routes across the desert and Indian Ocean sea routes along Africa's eastern coast, had carried slaves alongside gold, salt, ivory, and textiles for centuries before any European ship reached West Africa's coast. Arab and Ottoman traders, along with the Barbary corsairs of North Africa (pirates who raided European coastal towns and shipping for captives, including thousands of Europeans taken as slaves in North Africa between the 1500s and 1700s), operated substantial slave-trading networks that had nothing to do with the Americas at all.

Slavery and Serfdom: Related, Not Identical

Medieval European serfdom, which you may have encountered in earlier history study, shared some features with slavery: serfs owed compulsory labor and could not freely leave their lord's land, but differed in important ways. Serfs typically could not be bought and sold as individual property separate from the land they worked, generally retained certain customary rights, and their status, while inherited, was tied to a place rather than to their person the way chattel slavery treated a slave as property that could be sold, moved, and inherited independent of any land. Across many African societies before extensive European contact, forms of slavery existed too, but frequently functioned differently from either serfdom or the system that would develop in the Americas: slave status in some African societies could be temporary, tied to debt or captivity in war, and did not always pass automatically and permanently to a person's children the way chattel slavery in the Americas would come to require by law.

💡 Helpful to Know
This comparison matters for a specific reason: it lets you ask a sharper historical question than simply "did slavery exist before Europeans arrived?" (yes, widely) and instead ask "what specifically changed when European traders entered existing African trade networks, and at what scale?" That is the question this week's lessons are built around.

Where European Traders Entered an Existing System

When Portuguese ships began reaching the West African coast in the fifteenth century, they did not invent slave trading in Africa; they made contact with existing African kingdoms and trading networks, including powerful states like Benin, Dahomey, and the Kongo, that already practiced various forms of slavery and were willing, for their own reasons of profit and political advantage, to trade captives to Europeans in exchange for goods, particularly firearms and textiles. European traders rarely captured people themselves in large numbers; they overwhelmingly purchased captives who had already been taken in wars, raids, or judicial punishment within African societies, then organized and financed the transport of those captives across the Atlantic at a scale and with a permanence no prior trade network had approached. That distinction, contact with an existing system versus the invention of an entirely new one, is essential to getting this history right, and it does not make what followed any less consequential.
