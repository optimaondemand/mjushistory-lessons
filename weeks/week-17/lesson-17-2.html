Lesson 17.2
Philadelphia, 1787
Lesson · ~780 words
📖 Today's Focus
Delegates arrived in Philadelphia authorized only to revise the Articles. Within weeks, they were writing an entirely new government from scratch.

Fifty-five delegates convened in Philadelphia in May 1787, and James Madison of Virginia arrived having done more preparation than almost anyone else present, having studied the history of failed confederacies specifically to design something more durable. His Virginia Plan proposed a strong national government with representation in both houses of a legislature based on state population, a structure that obviously favored large states like Virginia. Smaller states countered with the New Jersey Plan, preserving the Articles' equal representation regardless of population.

The Great Compromise

The Connecticut Compromise (often called the Great Compromise) resolved this specific deadlock by creating a bicameral legislature: a House of Representatives apportioned by state population, satisfying large states, and a Senate with equal representation for every state regardless of size, satisfying small states. This structural compromise, more than any single argument, is what actually allowed the convention to keep functioning once the large-state/small-state conflict threatened to break it apart entirely.

The Three-Fifths Compromise

A separate, morally weightier conflict concerned how slaves would count toward a state's population for purposes of representation and taxation. Southern states, with large slave populations, wanted slaves counted fully for representation (increasing Southern political power) but not for taxation; some Northern states wanted the reverse. The delegates settled on the Three-Fifths Compromise: slaves would count as three-fifths of a person for both representation and direct taxation purposes. This compromise was not a side issue or a footnote to the Convention's real business: it directly shaped how much political power slaveholding states held in the new government for decades afterward, and it is impossible to honestly separate the practical political bargain that made ratification possible from the moral fact that it treated slaves as fractional persons under the nation's founding law.

Convention Compromise Builder
Choose a delegate position, read the opposing side's own argument, then reveal the compromise that resolved the conflict.

⚠️ Watch Out
Do not let the Great Compromise's relatively tidy structural resolution (large states versus small states) lead you to expect an equally tidy resolution to the Three-Fifths Compromise. The Convention also protected the international slave trade from federal prohibition until at least 1808, a further concession to secure Southern states' participation in the new union. These were not abstract debates; they were concrete political trades with real, lasting human consequences that this course will keep tracking into the sectional crisis and Civil War later in your study of U.S. History.
