Jefferson, Adams, and Drafting Independence
Lesson · ~750 words
📖 Today's Focus
John Adams later recalled arguing that Thomas Jefferson, not himself, should write the document that would define the new nation's founding principles, and gave two specific, practical reasons why.

Richard Henry Lee's resolution, proposed to Congress in June 1776, formally called for the colonies to declare independence. Congress appointed a Committee of Five (Thomas Jefferson, John Adams, Benjamin Franklin, Robert Livingston, and Roger Sherman) to draft a formal declaration justifying that resolution. According to Adams's later account, he persuaded the committee that Jefferson, rather than himself, should write the first draft, reasoning that Jefferson was the better writer and that Adams's own reputation was too "obnoxious" (Adams's own word) and politically divisive to lead the drafting of a document meant to unite colonial opinion.

A Different Kind of Argument

The Declaration's structure does specific, deliberate work. Its opening asserts a general theory of government's purpose and legitimate authority, drawing directly on the Natural Law tradition of John Locke's Second Treatise of Government, which argues that governments exist to secure people's natural rights and derive their legitimate authority only from the people's consent, forfeitable when government becomes destructive of those rights. The document then pivots to a long, specific list of grievances, framed entirely as personal charges against King George III himself, deliberately continuing the shift Common Sense had already begun, away from blaming Parliament and toward making the King personally, individually responsible for the colonies' complaints. This targeting of the King specifically, rather than Parliament or "the British government" abstractly, served the document's argument: a government that had "forfeited" its legitimate authority needed an identifiable party who had done the forfeiting.

💡 Helpful to Know
Congress voted for independence on July 2, 1776, and formally adopted the Declaration's final text on July 4, the date now celebrated, though most delegates did not actually sign the engrossed copy until August. The document's purpose was not to create independence (Congress's vote had already done that) but to justify it, to Americans, to the world, and to history.
