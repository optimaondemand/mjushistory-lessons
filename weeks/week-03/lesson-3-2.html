📖 Today's Focus
The beaver hat was, for a time, one of the most geopolitically important objects in the world.

European fashion demand for felt hats made from beaver fur created a trade network stretching from Paris to the heart of the Great Lakes. Beaver pelts, particularly ones already worn and softened by Native use, produced the best felt, and the animals were being trapped out of parts of the Northeast within decades of trade beginning. This gave the fur trade a built-in expiration date in any single region, pushing French traders (and the Native nations who depended on the trade for European goods, especially metal tools and firearms) to keep extending the network further west and further into the interior.

The Huron-Iroquois Rivalry and the Beaver Wars

The Iroquois Confederacy (the Five Nations: Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, and Seneca) and the Huron Confederacy were already rival powers before Europeans arrived, but the fur trade sharpened that rivalry into something historians call the Beaver Wars: a decades-long struggle, extending through most of the seventeenth century, over access to fur-trapping territory and to the European trade goods that access brought. France's alliance with the Huron, sealed partly through episodes like Champlain's 1609 battle, meant that from the Iroquois' perspective, France was an enemy's enemy, a dynamic that would later push the Iroquois toward alliance with France's colonial rivals, the Dutch and then the English, setting patterns of alignment that mattered for another century of conflict, including the French and Indian War you'll study later this quarter.

Missionaries Follow the Trade

French religious orders, especially the Jesuits, followed the fur trade routes westward, seeking to convert Native peoples to Catholicism. Jesuit missionaries lived for extended periods among Huron and other Native communities, learning their languages and, in many cases, developing complicated relationships combining genuine friendship, cultural misunderstanding, and religious conviction on both sides. Their detailed annual reports back to France, known collectively as The Jesuit Relations, are one of the richest surviving sources for both Native life in this period and French colonial attitudes toward it, written, like every source you've studied so far, for a specific audience and a specific purpose: to justify continued funding for the mission and to inform (and edify) readers back in France.
