Lesson 6.1
Three Colonial Economies
Lesson · ~750 words
📖 Today's Focus
By 1700, an English colonist's daily life depended enormously on which of three regions he happened to live in.

New England's rocky soil and short growing season never supported large-scale cash-crop agriculture the way the South's climate did. Instead, New England towns built economies around small family farms for subsistence, supplemented heavily by fishing, shipbuilding, and, increasingly, trade, carried on close-knit, church-centered towns where land was granted to groups of settlers together, producing the dense village pattern you saw forming at Plymouth and Massachusetts Bay.

The Middle Colonies: A Different Mix Entirely

Pennsylvania, New York, New Jersey, and Delaware occupied a climate and soil good for growing wheat and other grains at real commercial scale, earning the region a reputation as a "breadbasket" that exported grain throughout the Atlantic world. These colonies also developed unusually diverse populations by the standards of English America: New York had been a Dutch colony (New Netherland) before England seized it in 1664 and inherited its mix of Dutch, English, and other European settlers; Pennsylvania's founder William Penn actively recruited settlers of many religious backgrounds, including his own Quakers, alongside German and Scots-Irish immigrants. Port cities like Philadelphia and New York grew as commercial hubs, giving the Middle Colonies an economy built on both large-scale farming and urban trade.

The South: Land, Labor, and Cash Crops

Virginia, Maryland, and the Carolinas developed around cash crops grown for export, tobacco in the Chesapeake, and increasingly rice and indigo further south, on large landholdings spread across the countryside rather than clustered into towns. This dispersed settlement pattern, combined with a crop that demanded intensive, sustained labor, drove Southern planters' growing dependence first on indentured servants and, over the course of the seventeenth century, increasingly on African slaves, a transition you'll examine in detail over the next two weeks.

💡 Helpful to Know
Notice that none of these three regional economies was simply chosen. Each grew out of the specific combination of soil, climate, and available labor that settlers actually found, the same material-conditions logic you saw shape the difference between Spanish, French, English, and Dutch strategies in Week 3, now operating within a single colonizing power's own territory.
