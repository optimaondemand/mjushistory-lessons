Parliament's Response: The Intolerable Acts
Lesson · ~740 words
📖 Today's Focus
Parliament decided to make an example of Massachusetts. The other colonies decided Massachusetts's punishment was their own problem too.

Parliament responded to the Tea Party in 1774 with a set of punitive laws colonists soon nicknamed the "Intolerable Acts" (Parliament itself called them the Coercive Acts). The Boston Port Act closed Boston's harbor to trade entirely until the destroyed tea was paid for, devastating the city's economy. The Massachusetts Government Act stripped much of the colony's traditional self-government, converting the elected council into a crown-appointed body and sharply restricting town meetings, striking directly at the same New England town-meeting tradition you studied in Week 10. The Administration of Justice Act allowed royal officials accused of crimes committed in the course of duty to be tried outside Massachusetts, beyond the reach of local juries widely seen as biased against them. The Quartering Act authorized housing British troops in private buildings, not just existing barracks.

The Backfire

Consider a counterfactual directly: suppose Parliament had imposed the Quartering Act, or these punitive measures generally, only on Massachusetts, expecting the other twelve colonies to stay quiet and let Massachusetts absorb the punishment alone in exchange for being spared themselves. That is roughly what Parliament actually hoped would happen. It is not what happened. Other colonies, recognizing that a Parliament willing to gut one colony's charter and self-government could do the same to any of them next, sent food, supplies, and formal declarations of solidarity to Massachusetts at real cost and risk to themselves. Fellow-feeling among colonists, cultivated over decades of shared grievance (as you've traced across this whole quarter), proved strong enough that isolating any single colony had become, by 1774, a strategy that could no longer work.

💡 Helpful to Know
This response drove the calling of the First Continental Congress in Philadelphia, September 1774, bringing delegates from twelve colonies together to coordinate a unified response, including a colony-wide boycott agreement, the Continental Association. You'll trace this Congress's escalating actions in the weeks ahead, right through to Lexington and Concord and the Declaration of Independence.
